Overview

As urban populations and vulnerability grow, managing urban growth in a way that fosters cities’ resilience to natural hazards and the impacts of climate change becomes a greater challenge that requires detailed, up-to-date geographic data of the built environment. Addressing this challenge requires innovative, open, and dynamic data collection and mapping processes that support management of urban growth and disaster risk. Success is often contingent on local capacities and networks to maintain and utilize risk information, enabling policy environments to support effective data management and sharing, and targeted tools that can help translate data into meaningful action.

Building on the success of the global Open Data for Resilience Initiative, its work on Open Cities projects in South Asia, and GFDRR’s Code for Resilience, Open Cities Africa will be carried out in 8-10 cities in Sub-Saharan Africa to engage local government, civil society, and the private sector to develop the information infrastructures necessary to meet 21st century urban resilience challenges. The project will be implemented through a unique partnership between GFDRR and the World Bank, city governments across the continent, and a partner community comprised of regional scientific and technology organizations, development partners, and technology companies.

Following an application process, a small team of mappers, technologists, designers, and risk experts in each of the selected cities will receive funding, targeted training, technical support, and mentorship throughout the year of work to:

  1. create and/or compile open spatial data on the built environment, critical infrastructure, and natural hazards;
  2. develop targeted systems and tools to assist key stakeholders to utilize risk information; and
  3. support local capacity-building and institutional development necessary for designing and implementing evidence-driven urban resilience interventions.

Open Cities Africa will be carried out in the following five stages:

  1. Prepare
    • Identify OCA cities/teams
    • Finalize grant agreement(s)
    • Develop training materials
    • Establish analytics
  2. Assess
    • Hold Kick Off Meeting
    • Conduct in-country assessments
    • Define scope of mapping work
  3. Map
    • Create reference maps
    • Define daily mapping areas
    • Training of trainers
    • Collect field data
    • Analyze data
  4. Develop
    • Finalize problem statement
    • In-depth QA/QC of data
    • Develop pilot product(s)
  5. Present
    • Present results
    • Regional Gathering around FOSS4G
    • Leadership Training
    • Explore follow-up opportunities

Benefits for Selected Cities

Each selected Open City will benefit from:

  • Seed Funding – Small grants will be provided to competitively selected local organizations to work with municipal government institutions to address the identified problem through training, mapping and tool development.
  • Better Data – The collection and creation of up-to-date, high quality, accessible data to better understand natural hazard risk and support data-driven decision-making for your City.
  • Capacity Building and Engagement – Local institutions and selected implementing organizations will develop skills in data collection, mapping and tool development and will have opportunities to engage with other teams in the region. Projects will enable community participation and increase visibility of the municipal government’s work among local stakeholders.
  • Technical Support and Partnerships – The World Bank team will collaborate with the City to supervise and mentor implementation teams as well as facilitate connections with top technology and international development groups working in the mapping space.

Become an Open City

If you are interested in participating in Open Cities Africa, please work with your World Bank counterparts to:

  • Identify the natural disaster risk – Determine the natural disaster risk and related features (e.g., settlements, schools, etc.) that the Open Cities Africa project will address.
  • Select project district(s) – Choose the districts of the city that will be mapped.
  • Determine government stakeholders - Identify which government offices, departments or ministries will benefit from this project, and where possible, seek buy-in from those groups.
  • Determine local stakeholders - Identify which local stakeholders will benefit from this project and suggest local networks (e.g., universities, civil society organizations) that may be interested in engaging in this work.
